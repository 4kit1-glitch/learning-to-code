The acidity or basicity of a salt solution is explained in terms of the acidity or
basicity of individual ions in the solution. The following rules are applied to normal salts
that is, those in which the anion has no acidic hydrogen atoms:
1. Solution obtained by dissolving a salt of a strong base and a strong acid is a neutral
solution because when dissolve in water, no hydrolysable ion is produced that is the ions
that they produce don’t react with water molecules. So when dissolve in water, the
concentration of hydrogen ions and hydroxyl ions are not affected and so the obtained
aqueous solution is neutral.
Example 2.12: NaCl; when NaCl is dissolved in water, Na+ and Cl- are produced and these
two ions cannot react with H2O to produce H3O+ and HO- ions. In general, cation of group
1 and heavy group 2 (like 𝐵𝑎2+ ), and anions such as 𝑁𝑂3− and 𝐶𝑙𝑂4− will not affect the pH.
